IBC Chapter 18 and ASCE 7 require a clear picture of subsurface conditions before foundation design. In Rancho Cucamonga, that picture is rarely simple. The city sits on the massive Cucamonga alluvial fan, where sediments transition from boulder-rich debris flows near the San Gabriel Mountains to finer sands and silts south of Foothill Boulevard. A resistivity survey is often the fastest way to map these transitions across a large site. We run vertical electrical sounding to identify clay lenses, perched water, and the depth to coarse granular deposits that control bearing capacity. The method works well here because the contrast between saturated fine-grained soils and dry gravels is sharp. We typically combine it with spt-drilling to calibrate resistivity values against measured N-values, giving the structural engineer a defensible ground model without excessive boreholes.

On the Cucamonga alluvial fan, VES maps the boundary between dry, high-resistivity gravels and saturated clays that control settlement behavior.

Scope of work

Rancho Cucamonga grew fast after the 1970s, pushing development from the historic Route 66 corridor up into the older alluvial fan surfaces. Those older surfaces often hide cemented layers called caliche, which can fool a shallow investigation into overestimating bearing capacity. Vertical electrical sounding cuts through that ambiguity. We place four electrodes in a Schlumberger array and progressively expand the current electrode spacing, sending current deeper with each expansion. The apparent resistivity curve we collect tells us where the true resistant bedrock or cemented horizon actually begins. On a recent project near the North Etiwanda Preserve, the VES data revealed a low-resistivity clay layer at 18 feet that borings had missed between two coarse gravel strata. That layer changed the entire settlement analysis. When we need continuous profiling rather than spot soundings, cpt-test complements the resistivity data by giving us tip resistance and sleeve friction at the same locations.

Area-specific notes

The most common mistake we see in Rancho Cucamonga is assuming that refusal in a test boring means bedrock. On the alluvial fan, refusal often comes from a single boulder lodged in a matrix of finer soil. The driller hits it, calls refusal, and the engineer designs for rock. VES shows the bigger picture. The resistivity curve reveals whether that high-resistance layer is continuous across the site or just an isolated block. We investigated a warehouse site south of the I-210 where three borings all hit refusal between 15 and 22 feet. The VES profile showed a low-resistivity zone at 30 feet beneath all three locations. The refusal was boulders, not bedrock. Ignoring that would have placed footings on a material with far less lateral continuity than assumed. For any site in Rancho Cucamonga where refusal is encountered shallower than expected, resistivity data is essential to avoid a false rock interpretation.

Linked services

01

Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES) Survey

We run Schlumberger array soundings across your site to map layer resistivity versus depth. The output is a 1D resistivity model at each sounding point, interpolated into cross-sections that show gravel channels, clay aquitards, and the depth to competent bearing material.

02

Resistivity Calibration with Direct Soundings

We convert resistivity to engineering parameters by pairing each VES location with SPT or CPT data. This gives the geotechnical engineer site-specific correlations between ohm-m values and N60 or tip resistance, removing the guesswork from the interpretation.

Common questions

How much does a vertical electrical sounding survey cost in Rancho Cucamonga?

For a typical residential or light commercial site in Rancho Cucamonga, a VES survey with four to six sounding points and a basic inversion report runs between US$620 and US$910. Larger projects requiring more soundings or integration with SPT calibration fall toward the upper end. We provide a fixed-price proposal after reviewing the site layout and project goals.

What depth can VES reach in the alluvial fan soils of Rancho Cucamonga?

We typically investigate to 80 or 100 feet below grade using a Schlumberger array with maximum current electrode spacing of 300 feet. The actual penetration depends on the resistivity contrast between layers. In the coarse fan gravels, depth of investigation is excellent. Where thick conductive clays are present near the surface, the effective depth is reduced.

How do you convert resistivity values into soil types for foundation design?

We calibrate the resistivity model against direct borings or CPT soundings at the same location. The SPT N-value or CPT tip resistance at each depth is plotted against the measured resistivity, producing a site-specific relationship. This allows us to assign soil types to resistivity ranges with confidence rather than relying on generic published correlations.

How long does a VES survey take and when do we get the report?

Field work for a standard four-sounding survey in Rancho Cucamonga takes one full day. Data processing and 1D inversion are completed within three to four business days. The final report includes resistivity versus depth curves, layer models, and cross-sections tied to any available boring logs.
